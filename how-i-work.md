# How I Work

> **Coach. Entrepreneur. Sales strategist.** Now: the rare overlap where deep business judgment meets AI-and-software fluency — a cocktail that ships systems neither a pure strategist nor a pure engineer could build.

## I'm not a developer

I'm a business strategist and coach, not a developer. AI didn't replace a skill I lacked — it **unlocked** one. Now I turn strategy directly into working software, with no translation layer between knowing what to build and building it.

For 15 years I built businesses and coached people:

- Founded and ran multiple SMEs from zero to operating company (security, services)
- Coached real-estate agents, sales teams, and founders on strategy, sales, and growth mindset
- Drove commercial and marketing strategy as Commercial Director and as an independent consultant

None of that involved writing code. All of it involved the harder thing: understanding what a business actually needs, structuring the problem, and getting the right thing built or sold.

## The cocktail

Most people sit on one side of a wall:

```
   Business strategists ──┐
   founders · coaches     ├──►   THE OVERLAP   ◄── I work here
   commercial leaders     │      (AI made it productive)
                          │
   Software builders   ───┘
   engineers · architects
```

The overlap was always valuable. It was rarely *productive* — a strategist who couldn't build, or a builder who didn't understand the business, each hit a wall. AI removed the wall on my side. The three legs:

```
Business strategy + entrepreneurship  ×  Coaching / requirement extraction  ×  AI + software structuring
  (what to build, and why it matters)    (getting the real need out of          (building it correctly,
                                          people — and out of myself)            no dev handoff)
```

The coaching leg is not decoration. **Extracting the real requirement** — from a stakeholder, a team, or your own half-formed idea — is the single hardest part of building the right software. It's a coaching skill before it's a technical one. Fifteen years of motivational interviewing and sales coaching turn out to be exactly the skill that makes AI-assisted building work.

## The bottleneck was never the typing

Classic software delivery loses fidelity at every handoff:

```
business knows the need
   → writes requirements        (loss)
      → analyst translates       (loss)
         → developer interprets  (loss)
            → builds the wrong thing
               → rebuild
```

Industry data (Standish CHAOS reports) traces ~40% of software-project failure to requirements and communication — **not** technical difficulty. The expensive part was never the code. It was the distance between knowing what to build and building it.

I collapse that distance into one head. I am the stakeholder, the strategist, the requirement-extractor, and — via AI — the builder. No handoff. No telephone game. No "the business wanted X, we built Y."

That's why the work in this repo got built fast: not because I type quickly, but because there's no translation layer to lose fidelity in.

## AI is the enabler, not the threat

AI commoditizes *writing code*. It does **not** commoditize *knowing what to build*.

- An AI-augmented agency still carries the full translation tax — they don't know your business.
- A business person with AI but no technical judgment ships toys that don't survive production.
- The fusion of both halves is the scarce thing — and AI makes it **possible**, not cheaper.

## Proof, not claim

I'd rather show than assert. This repo documents real production systems — a 136K-LOC platform, a semantic-memory search system, autonomous multi-PR build pipelines, PDF-extraction layers — built in months, solo, with AI.

## Honest edges

I'd rather you hear these from me than discover them:

- **Speed came with iteration, not perfection.** The tempo produced a defect rate; quality came from fixing fast, not from getting everything right the first time. That's a massive net win — but it isn't flawless-at-speed, and I won't pretend it is.
- **The scarcity is time-sensitive.** The "business + AI" professional is becoming a recognized category. Today it's rare. In a few years it's more common. The edge is real *now*.
- **The fusion lives in one head.** That's both the strength and the risk. It's not yet a system you can hire three of. Part of what interests me is making it teachable — which is, again, a coaching problem.

## If we work together

I'm not the person you hire to write your backend. I'm the person you bring in when:

- you have a business problem and the off-the-shelf software almost — but doesn't quite — fit
- you need someone who can sit with your team, extract what's actually needed, and turn it into working software without a six-month requirements phase
- you want commercial judgment baked *into* the build, not bolted on afterward

The code is the easy part now. The judgment about what to build is the whole game — and that's the part I spent 15 years learning before AI made the rest possible.
